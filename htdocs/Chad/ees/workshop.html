PRESENTATION:

Mesoscale Model Climate Simulations: Advantages and Disadvantages for Impacts Assessment

Tom Wigley
NCAR/CGD

This presentation raised a number of questions: How good are climate models? (A subsidiary question is "how good do the models need to be?") Downscaling procedures depend on how well the global model can simulate atmospheric circulation as a driver for statistical or dynamic downscaling. AMIP results (from IPCC) show models are not particularly good at simulating MSL pressure and its year-to-year variability. How should we validate regional models; what are the needs of impacts models? Coupled ocean-atmosphere models from IPCC chapter 6 show large errors (up to 10-15 degrees C temperature error, 200% for precipitation for scales of 300-3000 km) with large model- to-model variability. The NCAR CSM appears to do well. Regional climate models at about 50 km resolution with boundary conditions constrained to be correct show seasonal mean temperature errors up to 2 degrees C and seasonal mean precipitation errors up to 40%, but this is not a clean test because of boundary-condition constraints (from IPCC). Results from the nested regional climate model of Great Britain's Hadley Centre represent the general spatial pattern, though absolute values can show large errors.

How do we validate regional climate models given boundary constraints? A naive approach could be: develop regression relationships between boundary and interior as a baseline; then ask, can a regional climate model beat this? Another problem is "uncertainty propagation". High resolution boundary conditions reduce interior error but then do we gain much from using the regional climate model? What if boundaries are erroneous? Another issue: how does boundary error affect the interior simulation? A possible resolution is to add errors to boundaries in present-day simulations and evaluate their effects. Impacts studies need primary variables (max/min temp, wind speed, etc.), so we need to test how well models reproduce these primary variables. Required time resolution will vary depending on application. We may need ensembles of climate-impacts results; maybe extrapolate from a single realization. What spatial scales are needed? Signal-to-noise ratio deteriorates as the spatial scale decreases. To what extent do we really care about very small-scale impacts: can we aggregate to reduce errors? Statistical downscaling may be as or more useful because they (1) easily allow multiple realizations, (2) preserve spatio-temporal autocorrelation structure and inter-variable correlation, and (3) are computationally less demanding.

PRESENTATION:

Impacts Assessments using Mesoscale Climate Simulations: Experiences at NCAR

Linda Mearns
NCAR/ESIG

These studies mainly used F. Giorgi's Regional Climate Model (RegCM). Topics included comparative spatial scales of impacts (mesoscale vs GCM) and incorporation of changes in climatic variability. Studies included NIGEC Great Plains center study, MECCA, NASA/EPA, NASA, NSF-MMIA, NOAA (Great Lakes; developed before its time). In the effect of spatial scales on validation of crop models (EPIC-corn model), there seems to be a critical threshold scale at which the crop model validates well. RegCM is used for high frequency climate variability. Two assertions are: 1) less uncertainty in validation because high-order moments change rapidly over space and less scale mismatch with observations, and 2) clearer signals in perturbed-versus -control. For the MECCA study, the RegCM was nested within the GENESIS model of S. Thompson and D. Pollard (NCAR). Using CERES-Wheat for a single point near Goodland KS, variability changes are potentially important in agriculture impacts. A stochastic generator was used to stimulate short term variability, particularly in precipitation. Important goals are providing scenarios to other NIGEC investigators with guide for appropriate use, annotation of scenarios (e.g., likely errors), and compare RegCM results with statistically downscaled results. The CSIRO R21 GCM also has been coupled with RegCM. The RegCM model overpredicts the frequency of light rain events. Variance of daily radiation also is much higher in the model than in observations. The spatial pattern of yields is sometimes good and sometimes poor. Conducting this kind of research is harder than expected because of the following problems: funding is difficult for interdisciplinary research; there is a need for close collaborations among modelers, scenario formers and impacts researchers; it is difficult to specify validation and development of criteria for acceptably accurate control run; determining relative "validity" of different variables; specific recommendations for responsible use of model output; and that model detail doesn't equate to greater accuracy.

Mearns: Yes, by late spring.

PANEL DISCUSSION:

B. Kuo (NCAR/MMM) and B. Machenhauer (MPI)

Kuo: Limitations for mesoscale models include limited horizontal domain, and sensitivity to lateral boundary conditions (BC). Model systematic error and "climate drift" often are not known (because of changes in setup from one application to another). The model climate is strongly dictated by lateral BC from the driving model. Opportunities include better regional climate simulations, improve physical parameterizations in GCMs, study impact of physics or numerics on climate. Coupling usually is one-way, i.e. mesoscale model is a sophisticated dynamical interpolator. Can the mesoscale model feed back to the GCM? There has recently been developed a coupled atmosphere-ocean version of MM5. Other problems include differences between short-term and climate simulations (sensitivity to initial condition, choice of physical parameterization). Nesting MM5 can give a cloud model within a regional model. Terrain height is much better preserved in high-resolution grids.

Kuo showed numerous examples illustrating these points. CCM2 radiation produced much better results for the East Asian monsoon (compared with Dudhia radiation), but replacing the Grell convective parameterization with Kain-Fritsch greatly improved the Dudhia results. There is a clear need to quantify systematic biases in mesoscale climate models, and to evaluate the ability of mesoscale models to simulate present climate before performing simulations of projected climate. Other goals are to assess the value and importance of two-way interactive nesting and to explore coupled atmosphere-ocean mesoscale models for regional climate.

Machenhauer discussed limited area models DK-LAM and HIRHAM. Simulations depend greatly on physical parameterizations. Higher resolution in the global model can produce worse results in surface pressure (e.g., exacerbation of negative pressure errors because of more developed cyclones). Unresolved questions include: How do mesoscale simulations differ from global simulations? How do climate simulations differ from several-day simulations? Feedback regimes can amplify errors (soil dries out, causing reduced precipitation, so that the soil dries more).

SUMMARIES FROM THE FIRST BREAK-OUT GROUP DISCUSSIONS

These questions formed a starting point for discussion:

  • What mesoscale model output must be archived for impacts assessment?
  • How frequently should the model simulation be sampled?
  • What cumulative output must be archived?
  • What are the error estimates for the model output?
  • How much do these errors contaminate the impact assessments?
  • Are there assessments that can not be done at the current state-of-the-science modeling?
  • Should known biases in model output be adjusted, even though this may upset the physical consistency of the output?

The PIRCS dataset was offered as a starting point.

Group 1 (Chair - Carmichael): The Biosphere Aspects of the Hydrologic Cylcle (BAHC) program has produced a list of spatial and temporal resolutions and data requirements that are common to ecological and hydrological modeling (Table 1, BAHC Focus 4, 1993). Items on this list produced by mesoscale models are precipitation amounts and type, daily minimum/maximum air temperature, global and net solar radiation, vapor-pressure deficit, wind speed, and pressure. All variables should be for the surface (editors' note: care must be taken to specify what is meant by 'surface' and descriptions need to be given on how some variables (e.g., wind speed and temperature) were obtained at the specified level). Target horizontal resolution is 10 km. Daily variables are preferred, although monthly values may be sufficient for some impacts studies. The group suggested additions motivated by health impacts: nighttime temperatures, rainfall intensities, water-flow and air-flow stagnation periods, atmospheric boundary-layer inversion heights, and cloud cover. Additional quantities are suggested by agricultural concerns: timing of freeze, dew duration, runoff, soil moisture. Some of these variables, however, might be better obtained from off-line computations in which model output is post-processed for the desired fields. Any calculations producing output not directly computed by the model for its own simulation must be carefully annotated. The group also suggested some standards for mesoscale model comparisons to aid impacts assessors: annotation of model parameterizations, and the length and number of runs needed to get representative statistics. The group also asked some additional questions: How does one derive necessary statistics from short-time regional simulations? Is there too much attention on internal mesoscale-model processes and initialization versus treatment of boundary conditions?

Group 2 (Chair - Strzepek): Many of the group's comments mirror those reported for Group 1. In addition, particular emphasis was placed on the desire for modelers to archive surface variables at least daily. Also, desired spatial resolutions are regionally dependent and are based in part on the need to resolve land-use and topographic patterns. Simulations used for impacts assessment need to be long enough to capture extreme events. It is much more important to increase the length of simulation than to improve spatial resolution. Appropriate mesoscale model simulations may not become available in time for impacts assessment for the IPCC Third Assessment Report, so statistical downscaling is also needed. Finally, care must be taken when nesting mesoscale models in GCMs to ensure compatibility of physical parameterizations.

Group 3 (Chair - Arnell): Again, many of the comments mirrored those of Groups 1 and 2. Even if a modeler archives a field such as evapotranspiration (ET), it is desirable to give impacts assessors the information they need to calculate ET in their impacts models. (Such an off-line calculation may also provide a useful check on mesoscale model performance.) Reliable estimates of changes in variability as well as means are needed when comparing "control" and "perturbed" model runs. In addition, it was recommended that modelers archive their clear-sky radiative fluxes at the top and bottom of the atmosphere, as well as cloud cover. These fields are needed to assess biases in model radiative forcing. The impacts community needs to know meaning and units of mesoscale model outputs in order to understand their limitations and use them properly. Two methods of using model output were identified: direct transfer of mesoscale-model output into impact-assessment models; and indirect usage, in which the output is post-processed, perhaps by using a weather generator or other statistical functions, before feeding into an impacts model. Error tolerances for impact studies need to be recognized: noisy high-resolution output is not worth having for impact models. This tolerance depends on the geographic area under consideration. Some assessments probably cannot be done using output from mesoscale models, because the length of runs for the foreseeable future will be too short. An example is the interdecadal variability needed for forest models.

PRESENTATION:

Development of a Regional Climate Model

Filippo Giorgi
NCAR/CGD

Regional Climate Models (RegCMs) have evolved from existing mesomodels such as the Penn State/NCAR MM4 through upgrades in physical parameterizations, especially radiation and surface physics. New routines are added by using interface routines to pass information; the parameterizations are changed as little as possible. The performance of new schemes is generally tested using real-world test cases in which observational analyses ("perfect" boundary conditions) drive the model so that output can be compared with observations such as station data.

Potential interfacing problems involving units, grid structure, call frequency, etc., must be resolved. Recognizing the spatial-temporal scale of the intended application is important, as model performance can vary with climate and region. The choice of new parameterizations can be driven by need to improve model performance and/or computational efficiency; e.g., Holtslag PBL to reduce accumulation of moisture in low levels. Model application to new regions or types of studies can also drive implementation of new processes, as can maximization of consistency with a driving GCM. Some aspects of parameterizations may also need to be modified for long runs or high resolution. However, newer and presumably better schemes do not always lead to improved results without proper tuning. In addition, a mesoscale model and a driving GCM should have consistency in their component processes. They do not necessarily need the same parameterizations: schemes used in coarse resolution GCMs may not be suitable for high resolution regional climate models.

When implementing new parameterizations, extensive testing should be done over different regions, spatial resolutions and temporal scales (daily, seasonal, interannual). The use of these models for impacts assessment has provided useful feedback on integrated model performance. One should also explore model sensitivity to variations in key parameters in the new parameterization. Parameters may need to be re-tuned for proper interaction with other model components. Their tuning may also change with location. Giorgi and Marinucci have studied the sensitivity of parameterizations to resolution in the NCAR RegCM2. Resolution changes have effects distinct from the resolution of topography (these effects happen even over the ocean).

Upgrading parameterizations or adding new ones produces a hopefully more accurate, physically comprehensive and computationally efficient model. However, a bigger model becomes more difficult to maintain and, with every major change, the modeler also loses the experience base gained with the previous model version. The direction for regional model development is no longer only as physically based downscaling tools, but as entities in themselves.

DISCUSSION:

Question: Is there a simple formula to calculate projected computing time for a given domain, run length, etc.?

Response: Yes, for a given model it is fairly easy.

Question: What are systematic errors in your model?

Response: Performance depends on region and season. Adjustments can be made for specific applications but only occasionally are the adjustments generally applicable. The model described here tends to over predict frequency of precipitation events.

Question: Does input for adjustment use GCM output?

Response: No, tuning is done with observed boundary conditions.

Comment: Tuning for current climate may introduce errors for future climate simulation.

Response: Some of the schemes are extremely sensitive to certain parameters; for example, the Grell scheme is very sensitive to the fraction of precipitation that is evaporated in down drafts.

Question: At some point will regional climate models have a comprehensive set of sub-models?

Response: Yes. We are trying to follow the lead of CSM.

Question: The problem of time and spatial scale becomes more complex. Can errors be attributable to numerical problems as well as physical parameterizations?

Response: Yes, although I have looked mainly at physics. For example, the Kuo scheme depends on the delta-x used to compute moisture convergence.

SUMMARIES FROM THE SECOND BREAK-OUT GROUP DISCUSSIONS

These questions formed a starting point for discussion:

  • What are the next candidates for internalization?
  • Are there specific input needs for a process module that are best satisfied by direct coupling?
  • What new, potentially significant feedbacks are introduced?
  • What are the impediments to direct coupling of a process?
  • What is the computational burden of internalizing a process? Does internalizing allow one to obtain an impact assessment more efficiently?
  • What other costs and advantages are there to direct coupling?

Group 3 (Chair - Potter): Regional climate models already include (for better or worse) surface-atmosphere interaction. Horizontal transport of surface water is needed to close the regional hydrologic cycle. Such upstream water is important for including wetlands in a model. It is also important to include human influences such as landscape changes and imported water (e.g., irrigation water drawn from underground). In this regard, one needs to know if the model's surface sub-model can properly accommodate land-use changes. A model may also need to represent sub-grid variability of fields such as land cover and precipitation. Other desirable sub-models include:

  1. Interactive vegetation models with growth that responds realistically to stresses
  2. Interactive water-body models (e.g., lake, coastal zone models)
  3. More realistic cloud-radiation models
  4. Chemistry models (though potentially very expensive)

On the last point, it was recognized that some aspects of chemistry, such as sulfur/aerosol modeling, may be more directly relevant to regional simulation (i.e., more directly interactive) than others such as carbon-cycle modeling. Before any addition, one should ask, "Will it make a difference in the regional climate simulation?" Off-line sensitivity study can help answer this question.

Group 2 (Chair - Lettenmaier): The group assumed that all models include a representation of the land surface and ocean reflecting the current state of the art. There needs to be strong justification for including additional processes. In particular, one must justify the added complexity. Off-line testing as in Project for Intercomparison of Land-Surface Parameterization Schemes (PILPS) should be done prior to implementing new routines in the model, followed by testing with the newly coupled system. Candidate sub-models for inclusion in regional models are

  1. Lake models (since they are not represented in GCMs)
  2. Water transfers and irrigation
  3. Parameterizations of land-surface heterogeneity
  4. Atmospheric chemistry
  5. Improved land-surface hydrology (this is already underway).

The group noted that land-use sensitivity changes may be done more reliably than integrated impacts assessment, since such a study involves altering boundary conditions with reasonably well understood changes. Although local changes in land are probably insignificant on GCM (global) scales, on regional scales, they can produce results as large as the 2xCO2 signal.

Group 1 (Chair - Vorosmarty): There are several immediate internal improvements that can be made, involving orographic effects, land use specification (urban vs non-urban, scenario formulation), and water vapor in the atmosphere. Assuming long-term (30-year) runs at 50 km resolution, there are several issues of importance to hydrology, ecology, agriculture, and human health:

  1. Heterogeneity, such as sub-grid variability of slopes, soils and vegetation. This issue affects runoff production.
  2. Horizontal routing of surface water, which is important for source/sink connections involving wetlands and oceans.
  3. Water engineering (e.g., reservoirs and draining or restoration of wetlands)
  4. Time variations in vegetation/ecosystems. Sub-models would include phenology, crop growth, and leaf-area index (LAI) and rooting-depth changes. Important seasonal, interannual, and interdecadal scales can be distinguished.
  5. Biogeochemistry
  6. Snow/snowmelt dynamics
  7. Atmospheric chemistry/aerosols, where there are several sub-issues:
    • mineral aerosol (dust)
    • sulfur cycling
    • NOx transformation to ozone and nitrogen loading
    • ozone
    • particulates (soot)
    • dry/wet deposition
    • indirect effects of aerosols on cloud structure and dynamics
  8. Oceans/sea-ice/lake models

The group felt that chemistry modeling involves the greatest additional computational burden, followed by ocean/sea-ice modeling. Modeling ecosystems and heterogeneity of surface features probably involves small additional burden. In principle, there are few impediments to improvements, but in practice, there are multiple purposes and agendas driving model changes. An integration of goals could improve the efficiency of model improvement.

PRESENTATION:

NCEP Mesoscale Climate Simulations: Recent and Planned

Song-You Hong
NCEP

Song-You Hong of the National Centers for Environmental Prediction (NCEP) gave an overview of the NCEP suite of global and regional models. At the global scale, the global spectral model is used for weather and climate predictions, and at the regional scale, the ETA and regional spectral model (RSM) are available for both short term prediction and climate studies. Coupling to ocean models at the global and regional scales are future extensions, as is addition of a runoff model at the regional scale.

From the results of simulations with the ETA model and RSM for July of 1988 and 1993 over United States, which were dry and wet summers over the middle U. S., respectively, both models were capable of reproducing the observed precipitation patterns as well as synoptic patterns associated with dry and wet summers over the Great Plains. However, in both years, the ETA produced too little rain with a cold bias while RSM showed too much rain.

It was emphasized that a recent improvement in the performance of the RSM was achieved by overcoming regional mass conservation problems arising from a mismatch in model terrain heights between the coarser, parent global model and the embedded regional model in mountainous areas near the lateral boundaries. The problem was overcome by employing an orography blending technique between the regional model and the global model.

PRESENTATION:

Planned Mesoscale Climate Simulations for TAR Impacts Assessments

W. J. Gutowski, E. S. Takle, and R. W. Arritt
Iowa State University

Bill Gutowski of Iowa State University presented an overview of plans for regional climate simulations for the IPCC Third Assessment Report (TAR) and shorter term simulations under the PIRCS community intercomparison experiments. Discussion ensued on specifics of simulations for the TAR. A question was raised about the possibility of using new emission scenarios. The consensus seemed to be that they would not be ready in time for global models to use and produce results suitable as boundary conditions for regional models. There is a school of thought that asserts that once regional effects are determined for one scenario, regional effects for other scenarios will scale with global effects. If results are available from simple (1-D) global models that might permit scaling of previous results, then effects of new emission scenarios might be incorporated into regional models. It is possible that by the time baseline verification runs are done with regional models, some results might be available from global simulations with effects of aerosols included. Although the region of focus for the planned simulations is the continental US where sulfate effects are primarily limited to the eastern region, it was pointed out that regional effects may be ubiquitous due to perturbations of the global flow fields.

The UKMO has produced global results with aerosols incorporated, but only insofar as the albedo is affected. MPI is making new global simulations that take into account the effects of aerosol being advected to other regions. The IS92a (1% per year increase in CO2 emissions) scenario is being used for these simulations.

A brief discussion was held on the PIRCS standard datasets. Encouragement was expressed to include cloudiness in the dataset.

Discussions on the length of simulations led to the conclusion that 3 or even 5 year simulations would leave analysts apologizing about weak statistics. Extension to 10 years would make a major difference. For hydrological considerations, 30 years in almost essential to be able to see sequences of floods and droughts. Running at lower resolution than 50 km in order to get a longer period was generally discouraged because key mesoscale processes become smeared out rapidly at scales larger than 50 km. The length of the run also is influenced by the spin-up time on soil moisture, which some estimate to be a year or more. It is important to separate model bias from spin-up, however.

PRESENTATION:

Future Emissions Scenarios

David Schimel
NCAR/CGD

Dave Schimel discussed the need for simulations that use scenarios leading to stabilization (as called for by the terms of reference of the Climate Convention) rather than unbounded increases of greenhouse gases. The second issue raised by Schimel was the need to consider the effects of aerosol. Present sulfur emissions are about 75 Tg/yr, but would go down if all nations adopt US, Canadian, and European standards. Direct and indirect effects need to be incorporated. The best situation would be to have simulations with two stabilizations, say at 550 and 750 ppm CO2, both with aerosol included. Some IPCC advisors are urging that the full report be replaced by a couple of interim reports. There seems to be no interest within the policy community in having reports that extend projections beyond 2050. Negotiators are very uncomfortable with agreements on stabilization at 750 ppm or above CO2. Mention was made of the paper in Science by Sarmiento and Le Quere entitled Oceanic carbon dioxide uptake in a model of century-scale global warming.

Current results do not consider the impacts of several feedbacks on atmospheric concentrations of CO2: effects from the biosphere are estimated to be 15%. Feedback effects due to landuse and ocean are unknown but expected to be large. In the case of ocean effects, for instance, the thermohaline changes may cut off ocean uptake with the result that an apriori assumed 550 ppm CO2 stabilization level might actually produce a 650 ppm stabilization due to ocean responses.

PANEL OF MODELERS: B. Kuo (NCAR/MMM). F. Giorgi (NCAR/CGD), S.-Y. Hong (NCEP), and B. Machenhaurer (MPI)

A panel of modelers discussed the details of regional climate simulation. One problem with the NCAR RegCM is that it produces too many small precipitation events. There is a need to develop a standard procedure for estimating the temperature. There generally is more confidence in the quality of monthly mean temperatures than in daily values. If users of regional model output know how the values were calculated, they have ways of creating subgrid distributions.

It seems that overprediction (values too large) of winds is characteristic of several models. There is a need to provide an overview of how good a model is. A resolution of 50 km does not give extreme rainfall but does simulate storm tracks reasonably well. Kuo suggested that another idea is to simulate a smaller region at very high (perhaps 2 km) resolution for a short period of time to examine some statistical properties from mesoscale models.

Hong emphasized the need to understand systematic errors and that statistical methods could be then used to reduce error for use in follow-on studies. The assertion was made that effects due to climate change are small compared to changes due to model biases. There was discussion on the possibility of focusing more on the changes than absolute values. Some thought should be given to saving variances at lower frequency rather than mean values at high frequency. Machenhauer pointed out that 0.5 degree observations give an underestimate of precipitation in general and snowfall in particular. High Resolution Hamburg Model (HIRHAM) uses subgrid orography to improve precipitation simulations. From the MPI experience, if the basic values of temperature and precipitation are bad, there is little incentive to have much confidence in humidity, wind, or radiation. Mearns pointed out that it is important to document how the verification was made. Potter encouraged that analysts make an intensive search for common, systematic errors in all models. Giorgi re-asserted that errors in the implementation of boundary conditions need to be separated from errors generated by the models.

Mearns observed that humidity values near the surface produced by the NCAR RegCM were surprisingly good, certainly much better than winds. Discussion concluded that relative humidity might be easier to predict. Changes in relative humidity due to climate change were observed to be small, and the group viewed this as something that might be expected.

Different methods were discussed, both internal and external, for improving the calculation of near-surface winds, which generally are predicted to be too strong. The PIRCS list of archive variables again was discussed, with encouragement to include clouds, perhaps high, middle and low.

The model verification phase (models driven by analyses) should precede any simulations with regional models driven by GCMs. One procedure is to examine ten Januarys and ten Julys. Encouragement was expressed to include Aprils and Octobers, but it was noted that sequences of single months do not capture seasonal transition effects. A question about soil moisture for such a verification went unanswered. B. Bass noted that 1994 data from the Oklahoma Mesonet and ARM CART site would be available soon for use in verification studies. Encouragement was expressed to use the NCEP data instead of single station values. F. Giorgi indicated the RegCM consumed about 180 hours of YMP time for a one-year simulation. C. Hakkarinen noted that the new C90 test period would permit large blocks of time to be requested, and that it might be possible to get as much as 30,000 equivalent YMP hours for a community experiment.

    Coupling Mesoscale Climate Simulation

    and

    Climate-Change Impact Assessments

    Atmospheric mesoscale models are useful tools for studying and forecasting the behavior of the atmosphere over several days. In recent years, scientists have begun applying these models to regional climate (continental scale or smaller) in simulations lasting several weeks to several months. These models can simulate climate for a region at resolutions higher than global models are likely to attain routinely in the foreseeable future. This feature makes them desirable tools for providing output on regional climate variability that can be used for climate impact assessment, because natural and human impacts of climate change are typically felt on the regional scales. Mesoscale models are thus complementary tools to global climate models.

    There are several challenges, however, posed by using mesoscale model output to assess regional climate-change impacts. Satisfying all of these challenges will require increased communication between mesoscale climate-modelers and impacts assessors. We categorize this communication into three modes of interaction. Rapid improvement of regional climate-change assessments will require concurrent activity in all three modes.

    In Mode One, the physical climate simulated by a mesoscale model provides input into impact assessment models such as agricultural, water-resources or human-health models. This is the most direct and immediate use of mesoscale climate simulation. Numerous examples exist of Mode One assessments using global models, which can guide mesoscale-model applications. However, a number of issues still need to be resolved, which can be summarized in part by a series of questions:

    • What mesoscale model output must be archived for impacts assessment?
    • How frequently should the model simulation be sampled?
    • What cumulative output must be archived?
    • What are the error estimates for the model output?
    • How much do these errors contaminate the impact assessments?
    • Are there assessments that can not be done at the current state-of-the-science modeling?
    • Should known biases in model output be adjusted, even though this may upset the physical consistency of the output?

    These issues involve challenges to both the modelers and the impact assessors. Modelers will likely have to expand the list of output they routinely archive, which increases data storage requirements and potentially slows model integration through increased I/O. Modelers may also have to build additional diagnostic codes into their models. Impact assessors will need to determine how sensitive their assessment codes are to errors in the input data derived from climate models.

    In Mode Two , mesoscale modelers add new computations to their model that are not fundamental to its simulation of climate, but desirable for impact assessment. Mode Two is a continuation of an ongoing trend wherein climate models have progressively internalized more and more earth-system processes. Recent examples include simplified representations of soil and ecosystem dynamics. These processes may be used in Mode One in external, post-processing modules driven by mesoscale-model output, whereas in the second mode, they are coupled directly into the simulation. Important questions for Mode Two include

    • What are the next candidates for internalization?
    • Are there specific input needs for a process module that are best satisfied by direct coupling?
    • What new, potentially significant feedbacks are introduced?
    • What are the impediments to direct coupling of a process?
    • What is the computational burden of internalizing a process? Does internalizing allow one to obtain an impact assessment more efficiently?
    • What other costs and advantages are there to direct coupling?

    This mode requires articulation by impacts assessors of what impact-assessment models are capable of coupling directly into a mesoscale model. It also requires modelers to articulate their simulation capabilities, such as what fields are routinely computed within the model even if not saved in output archives.

    In Mode Three, impact assessors upgrade their models in response to new types of driving data from mesoscale models that are not regularly available from observations. For example, many agricultural models use degree-days or daily minimum and maximum temperatures as input, partly because that information is recorded and archived. However, plant development involves temperature-sensitive processes during both day and night. Routine observations of temperature fluctuations during the diurnal cycle are not often available at mesoscale resolution, but a model can provide them almost on a minute-by-minute scale. Relevant questions for Mode Three include

    • What is the potential for improving impacts assessment models (when given more comprehensive information about the physical environment)?
    • Are there clear research paths for producing timely improvements?
    • What is gained?
    • What additional demands do such improvements make on mesoscale models and model archives?

    Mode Three can be viewed in part as a step that must be taken for a process before internalizing it in a model. However, it also poses a challenge to impact assessors that routine observations do not. It requires them to determine more carefully the sensitivity of their target processes to temporal fluctuations and whether or not their modeling could be improved with input data of much higher frequency or of a different type than routinely observed.
