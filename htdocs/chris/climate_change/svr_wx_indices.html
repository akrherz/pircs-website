Potential Climate Change Impacts on Severe Weather

Two types of severe weather indices are examined below. One is developed in the spirit of an ingredients approach to forecasting severe weather. We selected CAPE > 1000 J/kg and 0-6 km Shear > 10 m/s as thresholds that must both be exceeded to indicate potential for severe weather. Both thresholds are within the range of thresholds used in numerical simulations of severe convection and are above thier lower quartile values in climatological studies. The ingredients for a tornado are the thresholds for severe weather plus the 0-1 km Shear > 10 m/s.
The other approach uses linear discriminant planes developed in Brooks et al. (2003, Atmospheric Research). The equation for the discriminant planes are
  • severe weather: 2.86log(Shear0-6)+1.79log(CAPE)=8.36
  • tornado: 0.274(Shear0-1)-2.99e-4(LCL)-3.06e-4(elevation)=1.93
    In addition, tornado cases must also exceed the severe weather discriminant plane.

    The ingredients approach can be considered a more conservative measure, because it covers a smaller portion of the parameter space, and, thus, is less willing to accept a profile as either potentially severe or tornadic. On the other hand, the discriminant planes are computed for a data set of 3 years and can not be expected to have the same parameter values if a different amount of data were used to train it. Furthermore, NNRP data were used to train the discriminant models, yet they were applied to HadCM data. The good new is that despite these caveats the results from both methods are qualitatively similar. Though the number disagree, the conclusions do not.

    Main Results

    • The potential number of severe weather days increases over the Gulf of Mexico and the Midwest. Chasers of Nebraska and Missouri should rejoice! (Provided they expect to live another 50 years.)
    • There is a tendency for the main centers of tornado potential in HadCM to be located east of the NNRP centers. Furthermore, the inability of HadCM to generate low-level shear causes the maximum values to be greatly underestimated.
